Coaching

I created this page to offer coaching to anyone who has read my articles or watched my videos, and has already found my perspectives, ideas, and approaches useful.

If my information has been effective for you, then it goes to reason that you would likely also benefit from more nuanced and personalized guidance.

Personally I've always been very averse to any sort of coaching or courses. Online courses and coaching are like the breeding ground for overpromising and underdelivering. There is a trend of leading people down a rainbow bridge that guarantees miraculous transformations (and money).

It's not something I am a fan of.

So let's start with what I'm not selling as a part of this coaching offer, and why.

What I'm NOT Selling

Transformations

Coaches often brand themselves with explicit guarantees of transformation. Some even call themselves "transformation coaches."

The logic behind this is that transforming yourself and your life, your body, mind, emotions, identity, relationships, career, is a repeatable science of mechanical steps.

I don't entirely disagree. I do see most things as mechanical adjustments.

Take weight loss, for example. It's a dogmatic space online, with debates about how it works and which diet is correct.

One group says calories don't matter.

Another says only calories matter.

Another says it's about when you eat, so you should fast.

The truth is that weight loss has basically three dimensions: nutrition (which affects your overall health, hormones, hydration, digestion, inflammation), calories (which determine whether body mass increases, decreases, or stays the same), and timing (when you eat, how often, how long you fast, how long you stay in surplus or deficit).

With some personalization, these elements can be adjusted to affect basically anyone in basically the same way.

You could probably break transformation down into similar categories.

But transformation is really the maneuver of taking your energy, attention, and focus, and redirecting it from where it's currently going to somewhere radically different.

The problem isn't the methodology. The problem is that you can't control other people.

It would be one thing if we were talking about facilitating a materialistic, mechanical transformation.

If I promise that I can build a house for you, and I show you 10 houses that I have built, those houses act as proof that I can build another one.

This is a reliable promise. It's also priced in a way that makes sense: people don't pay for 100% of a home-building service up front. They pay a deposit, and the rest is distributed from start to finish. The process is very sequential and validated. You confirm a design, pay in installments, and all of my past houses serve as proof that I could replicate that creation.

Anything that targets personal transformation (like coaching) is a bit different.

Whether we're talking about sports coaching, life coaching, sales coaching, or personal development coaching I don't think it's reasonable to make promises about external results.

Even if you explain and demonstrate things perfectly, sometimes people won't understand. Sometimes they won't do it.

A soccer coach cannot promise that all of their students will get drafted into a top European league or shoot like Cristiano Ronaldo, even if that soccer coach trained Cristiano himself — because that's obviously untenable.

When it comes to personal transformation it's almost impossible to guarantee the changes someone needs to make. You can't control if they understand, what they do, or their environment.

Optimizations and improvements can be made reliably. But transformation is something not everybody actually wants, even if they think they do.

I'd rather you discover for yourself if, where, and when you want to radically change your life versus me selling you a dream.

I can explain how transformation works and how it happens. But I am not selling it.

Money at a Discount

Another common promise coaches make is that they'll teach you how to make a certain amount of money in a certain amount of time. Enough to quit your job. Enough to change your life.

This is a tempting proposition. As legendary marketer Dan Kennedy put it, you want to be "selling money at a discount."

If you could pay me $500 and I guarantee you'd make $10,000 in three months, you'd think: "I'm making $9,500 on this purchase!"

It's a wonder offers like these haven't replaced the stock market. That's a 1,900% ROI.

I've sold around $20,000 in total across maybe nine different freelance offerings. I'm not wealthy by any means. Some of those offers were doing business and marketing consulting, and I've gotten decent results.

I can teach someone how to sell, how to create an offer, how to put more attention into communicating with their clients/customers to learn how they can serve them better. But I have no idea what you'll actually go and do with your life.

Probably, if somebody spends all of their free time studying how business works, then tries to apply it, then goes back to learn more, then tries to apply it again, then goes back to learn more…etc, etc. they will gradually make more and more money. But I can't make somebody do that.

What I CAN Offer

Extra Mental Resources

Two heads are better than one!

Working with someone doubles the focus and energy on a problem of your choice. In theory, that should produce solutions faster.

It might also surface more problems — which generally isn't bad.

As Tony Robbins points out, "problems are a sign of life."

Real progress usually means taking on different problems, not eliminating problems altogether.

When two people point their attention toward the same goal it amplifies the ability to understand why things aren't working and build the way forward.

Asking Useful Questions

"Questioning enables us to organize our thinking around what we don't know" – Warren Berger, A More Beautiful Question

The above quote is actually why I chose the name "Mind Blade" for my branding. Questions are the blade of your mind. But they are like a multidimensional, amorphous blade.

Questions are how we unlock new directions and push our reasoning forward.

Studies on Socratic questioning, the kind of strategic questions that prompt you to examine your own thinking, show reliable improvements in cognitive flexibility, problem-solving, metacognition, and self-analysis. They shift you from passively accepting your situation to actively discovering what's actually going on.[7]

In coaching and educational settings, these questions reliably produce self-reflection sequences that lead to perspective shifts and sustained behavior change. I ask a lot of questions. Good questions, bad questions, stupid questions, smart questions, confusing questions. Different types of questions are good for refining ideas.

Space to Develop and Focus on Your Ideas

You enforce a time slot dedicated to focusing on and building forward what you want to build.

In a field experiment on weekly planning, participants who scheduled protected time for their goals showed improved cognitive flexibility and reduced rumination compared to controls.[1]

A study of knowledge workers who adopted daily or weekly focus blocks reported 23% increases in self-assessed output quality and 37% improvements in project completion rates within 90 days.[2]

Microsoft's internal research found that engineers protecting at least one two-hour focus block completed complex tasks 47% faster than those without protected time.[3]

Organizing Priorities

Probably the most useful mantra in my life has been "don't give up." A more practical way to say that: keep a relatively consistent top one to three priorities at any given time. These usually come down to increasingly specific focuses on health, mission/career, and relationships.

Research shows that people who prioritize based on urgency, importance, length, and reward become more efficient with practice. Poor prioritization leads to reactive focus on short-deadline tasks, even when longer-term high-reward work exists. Strong prioritization correlates with completing more high-impact work and lower stress.[4]

Knowing What to Do Next

The zen-yogi approach to life is that the best way to be prepared for anything is to be prepared for nothing. I usually find preparation helpful, but the point is valid: the next step is, by definition, the only step you can take. It's necessary to know the next step so you can take it. Sometimes you need help identifying or designing that step.

Research on goal-setting combined with action planning supports this. Brief action planning — specifying "what to do next" — increases commitment, self-efficacy, and follow-through.[5] In mental health and coaching contexts, collaborative goal planning with explicit next steps significantly increased goal achievement and sustained outcomes.[6]

What I offer is thinking clearly about situations, yours specifically, using whatever is actually relevant and useful.

There's no secret method.

No "4 Step Framework to Master Your Life."

There's just focused attention on your problems and honest reasoning about what to do next.
